# **Level 1: Practical AI Literacy — Complete Curriculum**

## **Curriculum Overview**

**Program Length:** ~13,600 words across 4 modules
**Micro-Lessons:** 20 total (5 per module)
**Format:** Self-paced, online slide decks (no video/audio)
**Audience:** Adult learners seeking basic AI literacy for work, school, and everyday life
**Level:** High school graduate to college freshman
**Delivery:** Presentation slides (8-15 slides per lesson, 250-450 words each)

---

## **Program Learning Objectives**

By the end of Level 1, learners will:
- Understand what AI actually is and what it cannot do
- Recognize AI systems in everyday tools and evaluate their impact
- Use AI tools effectively for research and information gathering
- Distinguish reliable from unreliable AI claims and marketing
- Read and interpret data critically without cynicism
- Use AI as a writing and communication partner while maintaining authenticity
- Make informed decisions about when and how to use AI

---

## **Module Breakdown**

### **Module 1: Understanding AI** (~3,250 words, 5 lessons)

**Module Goal:** Build accurate mental models of AI, move beyond hype, and recognize AI you already use.

**Lessons:**

1. **1.1 What AI Is (and Is Not)** (Myths vs. Reality)
   - Accurate AI definition vs. common misconceptions
   - Pattern-recognition model explained simply
   - Why AI is powerful but not magical

2. **1.2 How Modern AI Systems Work** (A Simple Mental Model)
   - Three-phase process: Training → Inference → Optimization
   - Why training data quality matters
   - Connection between data and bias

3. **1.3 Everyday Uses, Limits, Risks, and Ethics** (Where AI Works and Where It Doesn't)
   - High-value AI applications by industry
   - High-risk uses and ethical red flags
   - When humans must remain in control
   - Four key ethical questions

4. **1.4 Understanding AI Claims, Hype, and Marketing** (Separating Signal from Hype)
   - Why AI is surrounded by hype
   - Red flag phrases in marketing
   - Seven evaluation questions for claims
   - Self-driving car case study

5. **1.5 AI in Your Daily Life: Recognizing AI You Already Use** (AI Is Already Here)
   - Common AI systems: recommendations, predictions, classification, ranking
   - The hidden optimization problem
   - Critical questions about tools you use
   - Building awareness of invisible AI

**Module Skills:**
- Distinguish AI from science fiction or magic thinking
- Identify AI systems in your daily tools
- Ask critical questions about AI capabilities and limitations
- Recognize hype and marketing language
- Understand when to trust and when to be skeptical

---

### **Module 2: Digital Research & Information Literacy** (~3,400 words, 5 lessons)

**Module Goal:** Use AI tools as research partners while maintaining human judgment, verification, and accountability.

**Lessons:**

1. **2.1 Verifying Sources in the AI Era** (Trust, But Verify)
   - Verification pyramid: primary, secondary, tertiary sources
   - Why AI can amplify misinformation
   - Verification checklist for AI outputs
   - Cross-checking across multiple sources

2. **2.2 Prompting as Structured Inquiry** (Asking Better Questions Gets Better Answers)
   - Prompt architecture: context, task, format, constraints
   - Moving from vague to specific requests
   - Iterative refinement through follow-up questions
   - Avoiding AI overconfidence

3. **2.3 Avoiding Hallucinations & Misinformation** (When AI Confidently Gets It Wrong)
   - What hallucinations are and why they happen
   - Three types: factual, source, and logical hallucinations
   - Detection strategies and the citation test
   - Verification workflow for AI research

4. **2.4 Human-in-the-Loop Research Workflows** (AI as Research Assistant, Not Researcher)
   - Where AI excels (drafting, brainstorming, organizing)
   - Where humans must lead (judgment, verification, accountability)
   - Five-step research loop with verification checkpoints
   - Real workflow examples

5. **2.5 Evaluating AI Tools Themselves: Which Tools Are Trustworthy?** (Not All AI Tools Are Created Equal)
   - Categories of tools: general-purpose, specialized, search-based, domain-specific
   - Questions to ask about tool transparency and training data
   - Matching tool to task importance
   - Red flags in AI tools themselves

**Module Skills:**
- Evaluate source credibility in the AI era
- Write structured prompts that get better answers
- Spot hallucinations and prevent them from spreading
- Design research workflows that combine AI speed with human verification
- Choose appropriate AI tools for different tasks

---

### **Module 3: Data Literacy for Non-Technical Professionals** (~3,450 words, 5 lessons)

**Module Goal:** Read, interpret, and question data critically without becoming cynical or paralyzed.

**Lessons:**

1. **3.1 Reading Charts, Dashboards, and Reports** (What the Visual Data Is Really Saying)
   - Common chart types and when to use each
   - Data visualization principles for clarity
   - Manipulation tactics: truncated axes, color choices, missing context
   - Critical questions about any data visualization

2. **3.2 Understanding Datasets Without Coding** (What's in the Data?)
   - Dataset anatomy: rows, columns, data types
   - The representation question: who's included/excluded?
   - Selection bias, demographic skew, temporal bias
   - Metadata: the information about data

3. **3.3 Asking the Right Questions of Data** (Data Thinking for Better Decisions)
   - Four question types: descriptive, diagnostic, predictive, prescriptive
   - Correlation vs. causation (the #1 misunderstanding)
   - Importance of comparison and context
   - Question progression from data to decision

4. **3.4 Knowing When Numbers Lie** (Common Data Misuses and How to Spot Them)
   - Five common tricks: cherry-picking, misleading percentages, hidden subgroups, survivorship bias, correlation confusion
   - Incentive analysis: follow the money
   - Red flags in data analysis
   - Responsible data presentation

5. **3.5 Building Confidence in Data: From Skepticism to Smart Trust** (Using Data Wisely, Not Blindly)
   - Confidence spectrum: very high, high, moderate, low
   - Confidence checklist: design, source, replication, applicability
   - When to act on imperfect data
   - Avoiding extremes: data cynicism vs. data worship

**Module Skills:**
- Evaluate visualizations critically and spot manipulation
- Understand dataset limitations and representation biases
- Ask questions that extract actionable insight from data
- Recognize and expose common data tricks
- Develop healthy skepticism without rejecting data entirely

---

### **Module 4: Digital Writing & Communication** (~3,450 words, 5 lessons)

**Module Goal:** Use AI to improve writing and communication while maintaining authenticity, accuracy, and voice.

**Lessons:**

1. **4.1 Writing with AI (Without Sounding Like AI)** (AI as a Tool, Not an Author)
   - Why AI writing sounds like AI (patterns, phrases, structure)
   - What makes writing authentically yours (voice, judgment, specificity)
   - AI drafting workflow: prompt → generate → revise for voice
   - The revision locations where AI needs most work

2. **4.2 Professional Communication Workflows** (Structured Communication That Works)
   - Three communication types: persuasive, informative, collaborative
   - Audience analysis framework
   - Prompt construction for professional writing
   - Building feedback loops to improve over time

3. **4.3 Editing, Tone Control, and Audience Alignment** (From Draft to Polished)
   - Three levels of editing: macro, micro, line
   - Tone control through formality, language, structure, pronouns
   - Matching tone to audience and context
   - Self-editing checklist

4. **4.4 AI as Drafting Partner, Not Author** (Responsibility, Accountability, and Authenticity)
   - You're responsible for everything you communicate
   - When and how to disclose AI use
   - What AI can't do (verify facts, take positions responsibly, understand context)
   - The integrity test before sending

5. **4.5 Communication Across Platforms: Adapting Your Message and Medium** (Right Message, Right Platform)
   - Platform characteristics: email, chat, video, documentation, social, presentations
   - Choosing the right platform by permanence and purpose
   - Platform-specific mistakes to avoid
   - One message adapted across three platforms (example)

**Module Skills:**
- Use AI to draft while maintaining your voice and judgment
- Understand audience and tailor communication accordingly
- Edit effectively at macro, micro, and line levels
- Take accountability for all communication, regardless of drafting source
- Choose appropriate platforms and adapt tone to medium

## **Cross-Module Themes**

**1. Critical Thinking**
- Question claims and demand evidence (Module 1, 2, 3)
- Distinguish hype from reality (Module 1, 4)
- Ask follow-up questions (Module 2, 3)

**2. Human Judgment**
- AI is a tool, not a replacement for thinking (All modules)
- Verification is always your responsibility (Module 2, 4)
- You're accountable for what you communicate (Module 4)

**3. Context Matters**
- AI works better for some tasks than others (Module 1)
- Data is shaped by choices about what to measure (Module 3)
- Platform affects how message is received (Module 4)

**4. Transparency and Accountability**
- Understand how systems work and what they optimize for (Module 1)
- Verify sources and audit methods (Module 2, 3)
- Disclose AI use when relevant (Module 4)

## **Learning Activities by Module**

Each lesson includes:
- **Learning Objective:** What you'll be able to do
- **Key Concepts:** Definitions and critical ideas
- **The Core Idea:** One central insight explained conversationally
- **Real-World Examples:** Practical scenarios learners recognize
- **Critical Questions:** To ask when encountering similar situations
- **Reflection Prompt:** To apply learning to learner's own context
- **Key Takeaways:** Summary of essential points

**Suggested Assessments:**
- Reflection prompts (self-assessment)
- Real-world application tasks (e.g., evaluate a chart, draft a communication)
- Critical thinking exercises (e.g., spot the red flag, identify the bias)
- Decision-making scenarios (e.g., should we use this AI tool?)

## **Curriculum Design Features**

**Accessibility:**
- No technical jargon without explanation
- Plain language explanations with analogies
- Varied example industries (healthcare, retail, finance, HR, education, etc.)
- Cross-cultural examples where possible

**Practicality:**
- Every lesson connects to something learner does
- Emphasis on "what to do" not just "what to know"
- Checklists and frameworks for decision-making
- Workflow examples learners can adopt

**Engagement:**
- Conversational tone, not academic
- Real-world problems and case studies
- Reflection prompts invite personal connection
- Clear structure with headings and formatting

**Flexibility:**
- Each lesson is self-contained; can be reordered
- Flexible pacing (self-paced online)
- Optional deep-dive briefs can be developed
- Corporate add-ons possible for specific industries
