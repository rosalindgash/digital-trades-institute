# **Level 1: Practical AI Literacy — Detailed Curriculum Outline**

## **Module 1: Understanding AI** (~2,779 words, 5 lessons)

### **1.1 What AI Is (and Is Not)** | Myths vs. Reality
- **Core Question:** What exactly is AI, and what's just hype?
- **Key Takeaway:** AI = pattern-recognition software, not human-like intelligence
- **Topics Covered:**
  - Modern definition of AI
  - Common myths vs. reality comparison table
  - How AI differs from traditional software
  - Real example: email spam filters
- **Learner Reflection:** What pattern does your favorite AI tool learn from?

### **1.2 How Modern AI Systems Work** | A Simple Mental Model
- **Core Question:** How do AI systems learn and make decisions?
- **Key Takeaway:** Three phases—Training → Inference → Optimization
- **Topics Covered:**
  - Training phase (learning from examples)
  - Inference phase (making predictions on new data)
  - Optimization phase (continuous improvement)
  - Why training data quality matters enormously
  - Real example: Bank loan approval bias
- **Learner Reflection:** What data was used to train the AI tools you use?

### **1.3 Everyday Uses, Limits, Risks, and Ethics** | Where AI Works and Where It Doesn't
- **Core Question:** When should we use AI, and when is it risky?
- **Key Takeaway:** Context determines value; high-stakes decisions need humans
- **Topics Covered:**
  - Where AI excels: pattern recognition, repetitive classification, prediction
  - Industry examples: healthcare, retail, finance, HR, transportation, education
  - Where AI struggles: nuanced judgment, context-dependent decisions, fairness
  - Four ethical questions: Transparency, Accountability, Fairness, Consent
  - Real problems: bias in hiring, lack of transparency in credit scoring
- **Learner Reflection:** Think of an AI decision in your life—who benefits if it's right? Who's harmed if it's wrong?

### **1.4 Understanding AI Claims, Hype, and Marketing** | Separating Signal from Hype
- **Core Question:** How do I evaluate AI claims without being cynical or gullible?
- **Key Takeaway:** Financial incentives drive hype; apply skepticism to extraordinary claims
- **Topics Covered:**
  - Why AI is surrounded by hype
  - Seven red-flag phrases in marketing
  - Seven evaluation questions for claims
  - Self-driving car case study (real progress, exaggerated timeline)
  - Professional skepticism vs. dismissal
- **Learner Reflection:** Find an AI product claim online and evaluate it using the framework

### **1.5 AI in Your Daily Life: Recognizing AI You Already Use** | AI Is Already Here
- **Core Question:** What AI is shaping my digital life right now?
- **Key Takeaway:** Understanding what an AI optimizes for helps you use it consciously
- **Topics Covered:**
  - Four types of AI systems: recommendations, predictions, classification, ranking
  - What each system optimizes for (engagement, accuracy, profit, etc.)
  - The awareness gap (magic vs. mechanism)
  - Real example: YouTube recommendation amplifying extreme content
  - Four critical questions about any AI tool
- **Learner Reflection:** What's a daily AI tool optimizing for? How does that affect you?

**Module 1 Key Skills:** Distinguish AI from hype, identify where AI you use, ask critical questions, recognize when humans must lead

## **Module 2: Digital Research & Information Literacy** (~3,762 words, 5 lessons)

### **2.1 Verifying Sources in the AI Era** | Trust, But Verify
- **Core Question:** How do I know if AI-summarized information is accurate?
- **Key Takeaway:** Verification pyramid—primary sources are stronger than summaries
- **Topics Covered:**
  - Source credibility: primary vs. secondary vs. tertiary sources
  - Why AI can amplify misinformation at scale
  - Verification pyramid (6 levels from strongest to weakest)
  - Six-point verification checklist
  - Common verification mistakes
- **Learner Reflection:** Trace a recent claim you believed—where did it come from?

### **2.2 Prompting as Structured Inquiry** | Asking Better Questions Gets Better Answers
- **Core Question:** How do I get useful answers from AI instead of generic ones?
- **Key Takeaway:** Structure your prompts with context, task, format, constraints
- **Topics Covered:**
  - Vague vs. structured prompts (examples)
  - Prompting architecture: four components
  - Moving from vague to specific questions
  - Iterative refinement through follow-ups
  - Red flags in AI responses
  - Example prompts for research, decisions, explanations
- **Learner Reflection:** Write a vague prompt, then rewrite it with structure

### **2.3 Avoiding Hallucinations & Misinformation** | When AI Confidently Gets It Wrong
- **Core Question:** Why does AI sound so sure when it's actually making things up?
- **Key Takeaway:** Hallucinations sound authoritative but are false; verification is mandatory
- **Topics Covered:**
  - What hallucinations are and why they happen
  - Three types: factual, source, logical hallucinations
  - Detection strategies (citation test, specificity test, common sense test, tone test)
  - How to verify AI claims through source checking
  - Real example scenario: fake citation discovery
  - What to do if you find a hallucination
- **Learner Reflection:** Have you encountered an AI response that sounded right but was wrong?

### **2.4 Human-in-the-Loop Research Workflows** | AI as Research Assistant, Not Researcher
- **Core Question:** How do I combine AI speed with human judgment in research?
- **Key Takeaway:** AI drafts and explores; humans verify and interpret
- **Topics Covered:**
  - Where AI adds value: speed, summarization, brainstorming
  - Where humans are essential: verification, judgment, interpretation
  - Five-step research loop: Plan → Research → Verify → Synthesize → Double-Check
  - Workflow example: evaluating an AI tool for your business
  - Feedback loops and continuous improvement
  - Common workflow mistakes to avoid
- **Learner Reflection:** Redesign a recent research task using the five-step loop

### **2.5 Evaluating AI Tools Themselves: Which Tools Are Trustworthy?** | Not All AI Tools Are Created Equal
- **Core Question:** How do I know which AI tool to trust for a task?
- **Key Takeaway:** Match tool reliability to task importance; favor transparency
- **Topics Covered:**
  - Categories of tools: general-purpose, specialized, search-based, domain-specific
  - Strengths and weaknesses of each category
  - Six questions to ask about tool transparency
  - Transparency test: trustworthy vs. suspicious tools
  - Task-tool matching (wrong tool = wrong answer)
  - Real example: choosing a tool to evaluate a new product
- **Learner Reflection:** For a research task you need, compare two different AI tools

**Module 2 Key Skills:** Verify AI-provided information, write structured prompts, spot hallucinations, design human-in-the-loop workflows, choose appropriate tools

## **Module 3: Data Literacy for Non-Technical Professionals** (~4,667 words, 5 lessons)

### **3.1 Reading Charts, Dashboards, and Reports** | What the Visual Data Is Really Saying
- **Core Question:** How do I know what a chart is really showing vs. what it's trying to make me believe?
- **Key Takeaway:** Visual design shapes interpretation; always ask critical questions
- **Topics Covered:**
  - Five common chart types: bar charts, line graphs, pie charts, scatter plots, heatmaps
  - When to use each (and when it's the wrong choice)
  - Six critical questions about any chart
  - Seven manipulation tactics (truncated axes, color choices, missing context, 3D effects, etc.)
  - Real example: marketing chart with misleading visual scale
  - How to request full context
- **Learner Reflection:** Find a chart online and evaluate it using critical questions

### **3.2 Understanding Datasets Without Coding** | What's in the Data?
- **Core Question:** What does this dataset actually measure, and who does it include?
- **Key Takeaway:** Datasets represent choices about what and who to measure; those choices shape conclusions
- **Topics Covered:**
  - Dataset anatomy: rows, columns, data types
  - The representation question: selection bias, demographic skew, temporal bias, voluntary bias
  - Metadata: the information about data (as important as data itself)
  - Key questions about any dataset (collection, inclusion, sample size, timing, definitions, gaps)
  - Red flags in datasets (no date, no description, missing data, vague categories)
  - Real example: employee productivity data missing actual productivity
- **Learner Reflection:** What data is used at your workplace? What's missing?

### **3.3 Asking the Right Questions of Data** | Data Thinking for Better Decisions
- **Core Question:** How do I move from "what does the data show?" to "what should we do?"
- **Key Takeaway:** Different questions require different data; comparison gives data meaning
- **Topics Covered:**
  - Four question types: descriptive, diagnostic, predictive, prescriptive
  - Correlation vs. causation (the #1 misunderstanding, with multiple examples)
  - Why correlation doesn't prove causation (timing, mechanism, alternatives, experimentation)
  - Importance of comparison: temporal, competitive, goal-based, segment-based
  - Question progression from data to decision
  - Red flags in data conclusions
- **Learner Reflection:** Find a data claim and trace: Is it descriptive, diagnostic, or prescriptive?

### **3.4 Knowing When Numbers Lie** | Common Data Misuses and How to Spot Them
- **Core Question:** How do I recognize when data is being used to mislead?
- **Key Takeaway:** Five common tricks distort data; follow the money to understand why
- **Topics Covered:**
  - Five tricks: selective data, misleading percentages, aggregating unlike groups, survivorship bias, correlation/causation confusion
  - Incentive analysis: Who benefits from people believing this claim?
  - Red flags in data analysis (missing context, hidden methods, emotional language, incomplete pictures)
  - How to present data responsibly
  - Real-world examples of each trick
- **Learner Reflection:** Analyze a data claim for tricks and hidden incentives

### **3.5 Building Confidence in Data: From Skepticism to Smart Trust** | Using Data Wisely, Not Blindly
- **Core Question:** How do I develop healthy skepticism without rejecting all data?
- **Key Takeaway:** Smart trust means acting despite imperfection when risk is manageable
- **Topics Covered:**
  - Confidence spectrum: very high, high, moderate, low
  - Confidence checklist: design, source, replication, applicability
  - Four conditions for acting on imperfect data
  - Two extremes to avoid: data cynicism and data worship
  - Five-step smart approach to decision-making with data
  - Real example: evaluating customer reviews for a purchase decision
- **Learner Reflection:** Where would you place a recent data decision on the confidence spectrum?

**Module 3 Key Skills:** Read charts critically, understand dataset limitations, ask data questions, spot manipulation tricks, develop balanced skepticism

## **Module 4: Digital Writing & Communication** (~5,266 words, 5 lessons)

### **4.1 Writing with AI (Without Sounding Like AI)** | AI as a Tool, Not an Author
- **Core Question:** How do I use AI to draft without my writing sounding generated?
- **Key Takeaway:** AI is good at structure; you must add voice, specificity, and judgment
- **Topics Covered:**
  - Why AI writing sounds like AI (patterns, phrases, formulaic structure, formal tone)
  - What makes writing authentically yours (specific examples, conversational phrasing, imperfect structure, voice, judgment)
  - AI drafting workflow: prompt → generate → revise for voice
  - Revision locations: where AI needs the most work (openings, examples, emphasis, details, voice)
  - The authenticity test before sending
  - What to revise vs. what to keep from AI drafts
- **Learner Reflection:** Draft an email yourself, let AI revise it, then rewrite it. Compare.

### **4.2 Professional Communication Workflows** | Structured Communication That Works
- **Core Question:** How do I communicate effectively using AI as a helper?
- **Key Takeaway:** Purpose-driven communication with audience analysis beats generic templates
- **Topics Covered:**
  - Three communication types: persuasive, informative, collaborative
  - Workflows for each type
  - Audience analysis framework (who, their understanding, your goal, their barriers)
  - Prompt construction for professional writing (context + task + format + constraints)
  - Common mistakes (over-explaining, burying the point, audience mismatch, passive voice, jargon)
  - Real workflow example: business case for budget approval
  - Feedback loops and continuous improvement
- **Learner Reflection:** For a communication challenge you face, design a workflow

### **4.3 Editing, Tone Control, and Audience Alignment** | From Draft to Polished
- **Core Question:** How do I make my writing clear, appropriately formal, and authentic?
- **Key Takeaway:** Three levels of editing address different problems; you control tone
- **Topics Covered:**
  - Macro editing: structure and content (accomplishes goal? organized logically?)
  - Micro editing: sentences and clarity (short sentences, active voice, jargon, redundancy, weak verbs)
  - Line editing: words and tone (tone consistency, voice, word choice, rhythm)
  - Tone control: formality, language, structure, pronouns
  - Matching tone to audience and context (table with examples)
  - Self-editing checklist (content, clarity, tone, accuracy)
  - Real example: transforming formal customer complaint response to warm and accountable
  - Read-aloud technique for catching issues
- **Learner Reflection:** Take recent writing and edit through all three levels

### **4.4 AI as Drafting Partner, Not Author** | Responsibility, Accountability, and Authenticity
- **Core Question:** Am I actually responsible for what an AI drafted for me?
- **Key Takeaway:** Yes. You're accountable for everything you communicate, regardless of drafting source
- **Topics Covered:**
  - Accountability principle: You own what you send
  - When to disclose AI use (academic, professional credibility, paid work, when relevant)
  - How to disclose appropriately
  - What AI can't do: verify facts, take responsible positions, understand context, provide accountability
  - Red flags: when NOT to use AI (don't understand it, won't verify, hiding involvement)
  - Building AI into authentic workflow (think → draft with AI → revise → verify → own)
  - The integrity test before sending
  - Organizational implications and norms
- **Learner Reflection:** When would you be uncomfortable with AI drafting your writing?

### **4.5 Communication Across Platforms: Adapting Your Message and Medium** | Right Message, Right Platform
- **Core Question:** Why does the same message feel different in email vs. chat vs. a meeting?
- **Key Takeaway:** Platform shapes perception; match medium to importance and permanence
- **Topics Covered:**
  - Seven platforms: email, chat, video calls, documentation, social media, presentations, other
  - Characteristics of each (permanence, tone, audience, length, best use, cautions)
  - Platform-specific mistakes (email, chat, documentation, video call, social media)
  - Choosing the right platform (six decision questions)
  - How medium affects message (formality, seriousness, engagement)
  - Real example: one message adapted across email, Slack, and video call
  - Platform mismatch problems and solutions
- **Learner Reflection:** For a message you need to send, what platform would work best and why?

**Module 4 Key Skills:** Use AI for drafting while maintaining voice, understand audience and purpose, edit effectively at multiple levels, maintain accountability, choose appropriate communication platforms

## **Progression and Integration**

### **Within Each Module:**
- **Lesson 1:** Define core concept
- **Lessons 2-4:** Build understanding and application
- **Lesson 5:** Integration into daily life or advanced consideration

### **Across Modules:**
- **Module 1:** Understand what AI is and what it can/can't do
- **Module 2:** Use AI tools effectively while staying critical
- **Module 3:** Evaluate the data and evidence that underpins AI and decisions
- **Module 4:** Communicate about AI, data, and decisions authentically

### **Cross-Module Themes:**
- **Critical Thinking:** Question, verify, ask follow-ups (all modules)
- **Human Judgment:** Humans must verify, decide, and take accountability (all modules)
- **Context Matters:** What works depends on situation, stakeholder, stakes (all modules)
- **Transparency:** Understand how systems work and what they optimize for (all modules)
